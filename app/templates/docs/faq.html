You've got questions about {{ appname }}, and you want answers. Well here they are! If you've gotten here, but still aren't quite sure what the heck {{ appname }} actually does, you can check out our landing page which gives a good summary of what {{ appname }} sets out to do.

  • Why does {{ appname }} require a Google account? And what about my privacy?

    We use Google Accounts (such as Gmail accounts) for authentication for a number of reasons:

    1. they're free and easy to create
    2. almost everyone already has one
    3. they're super easy for us to use ({{ appname }} currently runs on Google App Engine which makes using Google Accounts for authentication crazy-easy)

    But don't worry! We only get access to your email address (not your name, or the birth certificate of your first-born child, or anything else for that matter), and we promise not to use it for anything evil. Seriously. In fact, currently we don't use your email address for anything other than telling that you're you. That's it.

  • Does {{ appname }} work with HTTPS/SSL websites? / I'm getting a "mixed content" warning. What gives?

    Yes! {{ appname }} stores your stylesheets on Google Cloud Storage which fully supports HTTPS/SSL.

    If you're getting a mixed content warning when you're viewing your website directly (i.e., not via {{ appname }}'s editor), your embed tags are probably out of date. Just log into {{ appname }}, navigate to your website's stylesheet, click on the "Embed" link at the top of the preview pane, and use the <link> and <script> tags shown there to update those already embedded in your website.

    If you're getting a mixed content warning when you're using {{ appname }}'s editor, this is expected and can safely be ignored.

  • Can I use {{ appname }} with an existing website? How do I get my existing styles into {{ appname }}?

    You definitely can! To get your existing styles into {{ appname }} you can either a) copy-and-paste them in (a.k.a., snooze-fest), or b) use our fancy-pants {{ doc_link('getting-started', link_text='importer', link_title='Complete with spinning chameleon!', anchor_name='import-existing-styles') }} tool (cool!).

  • What browsers does {{ appname }} work with? What about the editor?

    Stylesheets served by {{ appname }} should work in any browser that supports the use of the <link> tag (i.e., all of them).

    {{ appname }}'s editor supports the following browsers:

    • Google Chrome
    • Firefox 3+
    • Internet Explorer 8+
    • Safari 4+
    Basically, {{ appname }}'s editor should work with any browser that supports window.postMessage, but we actually test current versions of the above listed browsers. So browsers like Opera 9.5+ might work, but we don't really test with it.

  • Debugging minified CSS sucks, how can I make this suck less?

    At the top of your {{ appname }} stylesheet, just add @option compress: no;. This will prevent {{ appname }} from compressing your stylesheet (Note: this will apply to both the preview version and the published version or your stylesheet). Just don't forget to remove that line once you're done debugging things! If you leave it in, visitors to your website won't be served minified CSS, which could slow things down…and no one likes websites being slower than they have to be.

  • I can haz version control?

    Currently, {{ appname }} provides you with two versions for each stylesheet you set up:

    1. the published version
      • this is the "live" copy of your styles that is served to people visiting your website
      • this copy is minified and gzipped so that it can be served as quickly as possible
    2. the preview version
      • this is the copy of your styles that you can modify in the editor
      • this copy is what's used by default in the live preview pane of the editor

    As a side note, every single published revision of your styles is stored by {{ appname }} in its database; however, currently only the most recent published version and your maliable preview copy are exposed through {{ appname }}'s UI.

    We do have plans to add additional version control support (think along the lines of Mercurial or Git) in the future, but they're not ready just yet. Stay tuned!

  • Why doesn't {{ appname }}'s CSS use far-future expires headers?

    {{ appname }} stylesheets are in an interesting situation in that they might change at any time. All it takes to change the stylesheet your website's visitors receive is a quick click of the "Publish" button in {{ appname }}'s editor. Once that button's been pressed, {{ appname }} tries to get the just-published copy of your stylesheets out to visitors to your website as quickly as possible.

    To enable this (without requiring you to change the source code of your website every time you publish a new version of your website's stylesheets) {{ appname }} stylesheets are set to expire after 5 minutes. We feel this gives you a good balance between the cacheability of your {{ appname }} stylesheets and the time it takes for new styles to be served to your website's visitors after publishing a new version of those styles.

  • Isn't it bad form to put extra <script> tags in the <head> of your website?

    Technically yes. Ideally, you want to have all of your <script> tags right at the bottom of your page so that they don't block your page from rendering. {{ appname }} tells you to put both its <link> tag and its <script> in the <head> of your website. So, what gives?

    We do this because a) that's where the <link> tag needs to go and b) it's easier than giving you instructions to put one tag in one spot and the other tag in another spot. It also makes things easier if we ever change our embed tags and you need to update them. Basically, we wanted to keep getting things set up with {{ appname }} as simple as possible.

    So, if you like, you can put {{ appname }}'s <link> tag in the <head> of your website, and its <script> tag at the bottom of your website and everything should work just fine.

    The good news is that even if you don't do that, the JavaScript {{ appname }} adds to your website is very short and doesn't make any HTTP requests (unless you're looking at your website through {{ appname }}'s' editor), so it shouldn't make much of a difference in how long it takes your website to load.
